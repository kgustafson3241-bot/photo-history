The History of Early Photography

Before permanent photographs existed, artists and scientists used the Camera Obscura, meaning “dark room.” Light passed through a small opening and projected an upside-down image onto a wall or surface. Although useful for tracing images, it could not preserve them permanently.

In the Early 1800s, inventors experimented with light-sensitive chemicals. The Earliest Surviving Photograph is often credited to Joseph Nicéphore Niépce, who captured an image using a very long exposure time.

In 1839, the Daguerreotype was introduced, producing highly detailed images on polished metal plates. Around the same time, other methods used Paper Negatives, allowing multiple copies of a single image to be made.

The biggest shift came when photography became affordable and portable. The Brownie Camera, introduced in 1900, allowed everyday families to take their own pictures. This moment transformed Photography from a scientific process into a common part of daily life.

Photography Timeline

  • Camera Obscura: Image projection method
  • Early 1800s: Chemical image experiments
  • 1839: Daguerreotype publicly announced
  • 1888: Roll Film popularized by Kodak
  • 1900: Brownie Camera widely available
